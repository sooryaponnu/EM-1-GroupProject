GREATER COUCAL

Habitat

The Greater Coucal inhabits a variety of environments, typically favoring warm and tropical areas. These birds are often found in dense undergrowth, especially in areas like scrub, mangroves, thickets, and dense vegetation near cultivation and gardens. They adapt well to landscapes altered by humans, making them common in agricultural lands and village outskirts. These birds avoid very dense forests and are more commonly seen in open forest habitats. Their presence is widespread across Asia, from India to Southeast Asia, adapting well to different environments within this range.

Food

The Greater Coucal, a large bird in the cuckoo family, has a varied diet that includes a wide range of foods. These birds primarily feed on insects, caterpillars, small reptiles, frogs, and various small mammals. They are also known to eat bird eggs, nestlings, fruits, and seeds. The Greater Coucal is an opportunistic feeder and adapts its diet based on the availability of food in its environment. This dietary flexibility is one of the reasons why it thrives in a variety of habitats, ranging from dense undergrowth to cultivated areas and gardens.

Nesting

Greater Coucals are unique in their nesting behavior compared to other cuckoo species, as they build their own nests instead of being brood parasites. They typically construct their nests in dense vegetation or thickets, often close to the ground. The nest is a deep cup made of twigs, leaves, and grass. These birds usually lay 2 to 4 eggs, and both the male and female participate in incubation and rearing of the chicks. The nest, being quite large and loosely constructed, can sometimes be easily spotted in the undergrowth where these birds reside.

Breeding

The breeding season of the Greater Coucal typically varies depending on the region. In South Asia, for instance, it usually occurs during the monsoon season, from June to September. During this time, these birds are more vocal and display courtship behaviors. The male performs a display by fanning out its tail and strutting around the female. After mating, both the male and female participate in building the nest, incubating the eggs, and feeding the chicks. The eggs are incubated for about 15 to 17 days, and the chicks stay in the nest for several weeks before fledging. The nest is often a large, loosely structured cup made from twigs and leaves, typically hidden in dense vegetation or bushes.

Lifespan and mortality

The specific lifespan of Greater Coucals in the wild is not well-documented, but like many bird species, their life expectancy can be influenced by various factors, including environmental conditions, predation, and disease. In general, larger bird species tend to have longer lifespans, and it's reasonable to assume that Greater Coucals, being relatively large birds, may live for several years under favorable conditions. Mortality in young birds is typically higher due to predation and vulnerability before they become proficient fliers and foragers. Adult Greater Coucals may face threats from predators, habitat loss, and human disturbances, though they are known for their adaptability to changing environments.

Behavior

Greater Coucals exhibit several interesting behaviors. They are generally solitary birds or are seen in pairs, and are not particularly shy, often allowing close approach by humans. They are most active in the early mornings and late afternoons. Their flight is labored and heavy, and they prefer walking or hopping on the ground or among the undergrowth in search of food. These birds are known for their deep, resonant calls that are often associated with omens in many cultures. They can be quite vocal during the breeding season. Their ability to adapt to habitats altered by human activity has helped them thrive in a variety of environments.

The Kadalundi Bird Sanctuary,Kerala

The Kadalundi Bird Sanctuary, located in the Malappuram District of Kerala, India, is a significant haven for a variety of bird species, particularly migratory birds. This sanctuary is spread over a cluster of islands at the mouth of the Kadalundipuzha River, where it flows into the Arabian Sea. The Sanctuary hill, approximately 200 meters above sea level, provides an excellent vantage point for birdwatching. This sanctuary is notable for hosting over a hundred species of native birds and around 60 species of migratory birds, which visit seasonally. The avian species observed here include terns, gulls, herons, sandpipers, and cormorants. Key species that attract birdwatchers are whimbrels and brahminy kites. The best time to visit the Kadalundi Bird Sanctuary is from December to April, the peak season for migratory birds.Apart from birds, the sanctuary also has a diverse aquatic ecosystem, including a variety of fish, mussels, and crabs. The sanctuary's setting, consisting of expansive mudflats and serene surroundings, enhances its importance in the migratory bird conservation landscape. Boating facilities are available for tourists, offering a closer view of the sanctuary's rich flora and fauna, including turtles found in the areaThe Kadalundi Bird Sanctuary, set in the picturesque backdrop of the Malappuram District in Kerala, India, is a remarkable destination for those interested in ornithology, ecology, and natural beauty. This sanctuary, nestled at the confluence where the Kadalundi River flows into the Arabian Sea, offers a serene and invigorating environment for visitors.Covering a vast area, the sanctuary is spread across a cluster of islands which are dotted with lush greenery and framed by the gently flowing river. This unique geographical setting creates an ideal habitat for a diverse range of avian species. The sanctuary's rich mangrove forests play a critical role in maintaining the ecological balance

chimmony wildlife sanctuary,Kerala

Chimmony Wildlife Sanctuary, established in 1984, is a vital conservation area located in the Thrissur District of Kerala, India, covering approximately 85.067 square kilometers. As a part of the Western Ghats, a mountain range known for its exceptional biodiversity, the sanctuary is home to a wide array of flora and fauna, including several endangered species. The dense tropical forests of the sanctuary provide a habitat for numerous species of plants, mammals, birds, reptiles, and amphibians, making it a biodiversity hotspot.The birdlife in Chimmony Wildlife Sanctuary is particularly notable, with over 160 species of birds recorded, making it an ideal spot for birdwatchers to observe many endemic and rare bird species of the Western Ghats. In addition to its rich avian diversity, the sanctuary also supports various mammal species such as Asian elephants, leopards, Indian bison (gaur), sambar deer, and smaller mammals like the Indian giant squirrel. The vegetation of the sanctuary is characterized by tropical evergreen, semi-evergreen, and moist deciduous forests. This diverse vegetation not only provides a habitat for a multitude of wildlife species but also contributes to the sanctuary's aesthetic appeal as a natural retreat.
