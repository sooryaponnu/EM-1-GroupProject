Wynad day gecko

Appearance

The Wyanad day gecko has a similar habit to C. indicus. The basal part of the digits are beneath, with very small scales, and the two distal scales only being a little enlarged and plate-like. Its head is covered with very small granules, which are keeled on the snout. The upper surface of its body has large round granules, each with a raised central point or short keel, largest on the sides; these tubercles are either homogeneous or intermixed with much smaller ones, and as there occur specimens which are intermediate in this respect, no great importance can be attached to this difference. Abdominal scales are smooth. It has six upper and seven or eight lower labials. Males have four or five femoral pores on each side. The tail generally has a median series of transversely dilated plates below. In color, it is brown above, marbled with darker and lighter; the median dorsal line is sometimes lighter and dark-bordered; its underside is brownish, and the throat is brown-marbled; the tail underside is dark brown, and usually lighter spotted.From snout to vent, it grows to 1.6 inches (4.1 cm); the tail is 1.8 inches (4.6 cm).The type locality is "Wynaad and hill-ranges further south, in moist forests".

Habitat

The Wynad day gecko (Cnemaspis wynadensis) is a species of gecko found in the Western Ghats of India. Specifically, it is known from the Wynad region in the state of Kerala. These geckos are arboreal, meaning they primarily inhabit trees and vegetation. The Western Ghats is a biodiversity hotspot, and the gecko is likely found in the forested areas of this region.Their habitat includes tropical evergreen forests and areas with dense vegetation, where they can find suitable hiding spots, access to prey, and the right environmental conditions for their survival. As with many gecko species, they are likely to be active during the night (nocturnal) and spend their days in hiding.

Distribution

As of my last knowledge update in January 2022, the Wynad day gecko (Cnemaspis wynadensis) is known to be endemic to the Wynad region in the Western Ghats of India. The Western Ghats is a mountain range that runs parallel to the western coast of the Indian subcontinent and is recognized as a biodiversity hotspot.The specific distribution of the Wynad day gecko is limited to certain areas within the Wynad region in the state of Kerala. Geckos, in general, are known for their adaptability and can be found in various microhabitats within their range, such as trees, shrubs, and other vegetation.It's essential to note that the distribution of species can be subject to change due to various factors, including ongoing research and new discoveries.

Silent Valley National Park

Silent Valley National Park is located in the Palakkad district of Kerala, India, in the Western Ghats. It is part of the Nilgiri Biosphere Reserve.The park was established in 1984 and was designated as a national park primarily to protect the unique tropical evergreen rainforest and its rich biodiversity. Silent Valley is known for its incredible biodiversity. It is home to a variety of flora and fauna, including rare and endangered species. The park is famous for the Lion-tailed Macaque, an endangered primate species.One of the distinctive features of Silent Valley is its undisturbed and ancient tropical rainforest. The ecosystem here is considered to be a relic of the time when the Indian subcontinent was covered with dense, evergreen forests.
